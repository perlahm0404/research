## 15. Appendix: Philosophical Foundation - Why No CME Hours?

### Indiana's Unique Position Among US States

[FACT - STATISTICAL ANALYSIS] Indiana is 1 of only 4 states (out of 55 US jurisdictions) that do NOT require a set total number of CME hours for physician license renewal.

**The 4 No-Hours States:**
1. **Indiana (IN)** - No hours; 2 hrs opioid only (HYBRID MODEL)
2. **Montana (MT)** - No hours; no topics (PURE AUTONOMY)
3. **South Dakota (SD)** - No hours; no topics (PURE AUTONOMY)
4. **New York (NY)** - No hours; multiple topics (TOPIC-SPECIFIC MODEL)

**Statistical Context:**
- **51 of 55 jurisdictions (93%)** require total CME hours ranging from 20-200 hours per cycle
- **4 of 55 jurisdictions (7%)** have no total hours
- **Indiana represents 1.8% of all US jurisdictions**

### Three Regulatory Models for Physician Competency

**Model 1: COMPREHENSIVE CME (Most Common - 51 States)**

**Philosophy:** Continuous learning through structured CME ensures physician competency

**Characteristics:**
- Total hour requirements (20-200 hours per cycle)
- Category 1 minimums (formal accredited CME)
- Multiple mandatory topics (ethics, pain, cultural competency, etc.)
- Audit systems to verify compliance

**Examples:**
- Illinois: 150 hrs/3yr + 5 mandatory topics
- Pennsylvania: 100 hrs/2yr + 4 mandatory topics
- California (MD): 50 hrs/2yr + 12-hr pain management

**Advantages:**
- Verifiable competency maintenance
- Addresses broad public health priorities
- Structured continuing education

**Disadvantages:**
- Administrative burden (tracking, auditing)
- Cost to physicians (CME courses, time)
- One-size-fits-all approach

**Model 2: PURE PROFESSIONAL AUTONOMY (Montana, South Dakota)**

**Philosophy:** Physicians are trusted professionals who self-direct learning; state should not mandate CME

**Characteristics:**
- NO total hour requirements
- NO mandatory topics
- Professional responsibility for staying current
- Minimal state oversight

**Examples:**
- Montana: No CME hours, no topics
- South Dakota: No CME hours, no topics

**Advantages:**
- Minimal regulatory burden
- Maximum professional autonomy
- Low administrative cost
- Flexibility for relevant learning

**Disadvantages:**
- No verification of competency maintenance
- Public health priorities may be neglected
- Difficult to ensure minimum standards

**Model 3: TARGETED TOPIC-SPECIFIC (Indiana, New York - HYBRID)**

**Philosophy:** Mandate only critical public health topics; trust physician autonomy for general learning

**Characteristics:**
- NO total hour requirements
- LIMITED mandatory topics (public health priorities only)
- Balance autonomy with targeted intervention

**Examples:**
- **Indiana:** No hours; 2 hrs opioid only
- **New York:** No hours; child abuse, infection control, pain management (DEA)

**Advantages:**
- Minimal burden while addressing crises
- Professional autonomy for general CME
- Focused on specific public health needs
- Administrative efficiency

**Disadvantages:**
